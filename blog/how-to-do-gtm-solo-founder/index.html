As a solo founder at $50K-$100K MRR, effective GTM comes down to five repeatable motions: signal monitoring for buying intent, one LinkedIn post per week from a real opinion, two to three direct outreach messages to warm contacts weekly, one podcast appearance per month, and one in-person event per quarter. Everything else is optional until those five are running consistently.

TL;DR: Five motions. 90 minutes per week. Signal monitoring (15 min), one LinkedIn post (45 min), two to three warm outreach messages (30 min). Add one podcast per month and one event per quarter. That is the full solo founder GTM system at $50K-$100K MRR. Everything else is optional noise.

Most GTM advice is written by people who work at funded companies. They have a marketing team, a sales development rep, a content manager, and a budget for paid acquisition. They write about GTM as if those resources are a given.

You have yourself. Maybe 90 minutes on a good week. Here's what actually works with those constraints.

Why most GTM advice is wrong for solo founders

The standard advice goes something like this: build a content engine, run outbound sequences, invest in SEO, set up a newsletter, attend 4 events per quarter, do paid acquisition to test messaging, and hire a fractional CMO to coordinate it all.

That advice requires 20 to 30 hours per week of focused GTM work and a $10,000 per month budget to execute at even minimum viable volume. A solo founder cannot do this without stopping product development entirely.

The advice is not wrong in principle. It works. It just wasn't written for you. It was written for a company that has already solved the solo founder problem and hired its way out of it.

The question is not "what is the best GTM strategy?" The question is "what is the best GTM strategy that one person can execute for 90 minutes per week and still compound over time?" Those are different questions with different answers.

The 5 things that work for solo founders

1. Signal monitoring (15 minutes per day, or 15 minutes once per week for a summary).

Signal monitoring is scanning for buying intent from your ICP. Job postings that indicate budget for what you sell. Funding announcements from companies in your target segment. LinkedIn posts where a prospect describes the exact problem you solve. Competitor complaints in community forums. Executive changes that reset vendor relationships.

Each signal is a reason to reach out. Not a cold pitch. A relevant observation. "Saw you just hired a VP of Operations. A lot of companies at that stage run into [specific problem you solve]. Happy to share what we've seen work." That message gets a response. A cold email does not.

2. One LinkedIn post per week.

Not a company update. Not a product announcement. One short post from your actual opinion, grounded in a real decision, observation, or experience from the week. 200 to 400 words. One clear point. Published every week, same cadence.

This is not content marketing. It is credibility compounding. At 52 posts per year, you have a public record of how you think. Buyers who find you through a signal check your profile. If there's consistent, specific thinking there, you become a person worth talking to instead of a cold founder with a pitch deck.

3. Two to three direct outreach messages per week to warm contacts.

Not mass email. Two or three personalized messages per week, each one triggered by a specific signal or a specific reason. Sent via LinkedIn, email, or text depending on the relationship. Each one specific enough that the recipient knows you wrote it for them, not for a list.

This is the highest-conversion GTM motion available to a solo founder. Response rates on signal-triggered personal outreach regularly run 20 to 40 percent. Cold email response rates run 2 to 5 percent. The volume is dramatically lower. The ROI per hour is dramatically higher.

4. One podcast appearance per month.

Not your own podcast. Appearing on shows your ICP already listens to. One booking per month is achievable solo with a systematic outreach approach: identify 10 relevant shows, send personalized pitches, follow up once. Three to four will say yes over any given quarter.

A podcast appearance puts you in front of 1,000 to 10,000 relevant listeners in one hour. You cannot replicate that ROI with a LinkedIn post. And unlike ads, it doesn't stop working when you stop paying. Episodes stay discoverable for years.

5. One in-person event per quarter.

Pick one. The conference, meetup, or community gathering where your ICP concentrates. Show up in person. Have real conversations. Follow up with the three to five people you actually connected with. That's it.

In-person is where founder relationships form and trust accelerates. A 20-minute conversation at the right event compresses months of digital relationship-building. One event per quarter is enough to maintain momentum without consuming the calendar.

The weekly time budget: 90 minutes total

Motion Time per week Output
Signal monitoring 15 minutes 3-5 signals reviewed, 1-2 flagged for outreach
LinkedIn post 45 minutes 1 post live, compounding visibility
Warm outreach 30 minutes 2-3 personalized messages sent
Total 90 minutes Full GTM operation running

Add two to three hours per month for the podcast appearance (pitch, prep, record, follow-up). Add half a day per quarter for the event. That's the full time investment.

Everything else, the newsletter, the SEO blog, the cold email campaign, the social media management tool, can wait until you have a team or a reason to believe it will convert better than these five motions.

What doesn't work solo

Cold email at volume. Cold email works when you have a team to test subject lines, iterate on sequences, manage deliverability, and follow up systematically across hundreds of threads. Solo, it costs more time than the response rate justifies. Two warm outreach messages outperform 100 cold ones in an equal amount of time.

Building a newsletter from zero. A newsletter is a retention and authority play that takes 12 to 18 months to build an audience large enough to generate pipeline. At founder stage, that is not where your 90 minutes per week should go. Build the newsletter after you have a LinkedIn following to funnel into it.

Paid ads before product-market fit. Paid ads amplify what's already working. If you don't know which message converts and which ICP buys, ads will tell you nothing except that the wrong message doesn't convert at scale. Figure out what works organically first. Then amplify it.

Agencies that don't know your product. A general content agency cannot write with the specificity that generates trust in B2B sales cycles. Generic content produces generic results. If you're going to pay for execution support, work with people who spend the first two weeks in your product and with your customers before writing a single word.

When to hand it off

Hand off GTM when two conditions are true simultaneously: you are spending more than 4 hours per week on it, and it is still not compounding.

If you're spending 4 hours and pipeline is growing week over week, that's a good problem. Keep going and hire to scale what's working. If you're spending 4 hours and nothing is compounding, the problem is either ICP clarity, message specificity, or operating bandwidth. An agency won't fix the first two. But operational support can help with the third.

The right moment to hand off is when you've proven the five motions work at 90 minutes per week and you want to scale the volume without scaling your personal time investment. That's when a fractional GTM team makes economic sense: you've validated the engine, and now you need someone else to run it so you can build product.

How do you do outbound alone as a founder?

Solo founder outbound works at low volume with high specificity. Two to three messages per week, each one personalized to a specific person based on a recent signal, a post they published, a job change, a comment they made, a competitor they mentioned. Skip the mass email sequences. One good message to one right person beats 100 generic touches every time at founder stage.

When should a solo founder hand off GTM?

Hand off GTM when you are spending more than 4 hours per week on it and it is still not compounding. If you've been consistent for 90 days and pipeline isn't growing, you either have an ICP problem that needs solving first, or you need operational support to scale what's working. Handing off to an agency before you know what's working just accelerates the wrong thing.

What is signal monitoring?

Signal monitoring is the daily practice of scanning for buying intent signals from your ICP: job postings that indicate budget, funding announcements that signal growth mode, LinkedIn posts where prospects describe the exact problem you solve, competitor complaints in community forums, and executive changes that reset existing vendor relationships. Each signal is a warm outreach opportunity.
